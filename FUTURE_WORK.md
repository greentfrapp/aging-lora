# Future Work

Stretch goals and open questions beyond the Phase 1–5 roadmap. None of these are in scope for the current study; they are recorded here as Phase N+1 candidates.

- **Multi-tissue extension.** The current project is explicitly scoped to PBMC cell types. scAgeClock (Xie, *npj Aging* 2026) trains on >16 million cells from >40 tissues and >400 cell types; extending the foundation-model fine-tuning protocol to a matched multi-tissue corpus (e.g., Tabula Muris Senis equivalents for human, or the AgeAnno 28-tissue atlas used for pretraining-coverage audits here) would test whether the LOCO and few-shot gains observed in immune cells generalise across tissue compartments.

- **CellFM inclusion.** CellFM (Zeng et al., *Nature Communications* 2025) — 800 million parameters pretrained on 100 million human cells, outperforming UCE by 2.1% AvgBio on standard cell-annotation benchmarks — was excluded from the primary model panel due to MindSpore platform incompatibility with standard PyTorch pipelines. A community PyTorch re-implementation or dedicated MindSpore cluster run would complete the model panel and close the largest gap in the comparator sweep.

- **VAE baselines in the few-shot regime.** Inclusion of scVI/scANVI (Lopez et al., *Nature Methods* 2018; Xu et al., *Molecular Systems Biology* 2021) as deep-learning non-foundation baselines was deferred to project-start evaluation. Systematic benchmarking of amortised VAE inference against LoRA-fine-tuned foundation models across the full few-shot downsampling curve would clarify whether the "when pretraining pays" threshold is specific to the foundation-model pretraining paradigm or shared by any sufficiently flexible deep-learning encoder.

- **Dedicated cross-ancestry aging clock.** The AIDA holdout (Kock et al., *Cell* 2025; 619 donors across 7 Asian population groups) is used here only as an external ancestry-shift evaluation. A sibling project using AIDA as a primary training corpus — asking which foundation-model embeddings yield ancestry-invariant aging representations — would address the cross-ancestry question directly rather than as a secondary readout.

- **Integration with inflammatory aging signals.** iAge (Sayed, Huang et al., *Nature Aging* 2021) is the canonical inflammatory aging clock, trained on plasma cytokines rather than scRNA-seq. Matching a PBMC single-cell cohort with paired serum cytokine profiles would enable a direct test of whether foundation-model age-axis directions in the transcriptomic latent space align with iAge's CXCL9-dominated inflammatory axis — a question that cannot be asked of any scRNA-seq–only dataset including the five sc-ImmuAging cohorts.

- **Bayesian uncertainty heads.** scMLEAge (Hu, Pellegrini, bioRxiv 2025) shows that probabilistic age estimates with explicit sequencing-depth uncertainty can outperform point-estimate regressors in some cell types. Adding a Bayesian or conformal-prediction head to the best-performing foundation model from the primary study would test whether uncertainty quantification improves calibration under LOCO cohort shift — a regime where out-of-distribution coverage is hardest to guarantee.

- **Disease-state and accelerated aging.** The scope caveat explicitly excludes disease-state aging (e.g., HIV-associated accelerated epigenetic aging, autoimmune-associated immunosenescence). The benchmark harness released in Phase 5 — with frozen LOCO splits and version-pinned dependencies — is designed to be plugged into by future work; applying it to matched disease PBMC cohorts is the intended next use of the v1 infrastructure.

- **Maintained harness (v2).** The Phase 5 v1 harness is frozen and the original authors' obligation ends at publication. A community-maintained v2 with continuous integration, backward-compatibility with future scGPT/Geneformer major releases, and compatibility with new PBMC cohorts (e.g., UK Biobank single-cell initiative) is the natural follow-on infrastructure project — explicitly deferred to avoid overpromising maintenance in the original paper.
